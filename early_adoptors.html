Early Adoptors

Descartes Dubs "Imaginaries"

In La Geometrie, Rene Descartes, an early adopter of using complex numbers, which he dubbed “Imaginary,” seemed to have been influenced by Cardano's work because Descartes is finding imaginary roots as well as real roots, and he provides a method for finding these roots and checking them.

For any equation one can imagine as many roots [as its degree would suggest],but in many cases no quantity exists which corresponds to what one imagines.

Walis's Way with Geometry

John Wallis was far ahead of his time when he represented imaginary numbers geometrically with triangles on a circle. He showed that an imaginary number can be represented by triangles with negative lengths (left of the origin).

The Complex Plane Developed

Caspar Wessel developed the idea of the complex plane with an imaginary and real axis which we use today. He discovered using line segments with the properties of modern day vectors that the imaginary axis is orthogonal to the real axis.

De Moivre Dives into Complex Geometry

Abraham De Moivre created a relation between trigonometric functions and complex numbers with his formula z^n=(cos(Θ)+isin(Θ))^n=(cos(nΘ)+isin(nΘ)). He used many geometric arguments which explained many unique properties of the complex plane.

Euler's Expansion into Calculus

Leonhard Euler came up with the now standardized notion for the square root of negative one: i. He also refined the geometric definition for complex numbers, as well as created a way to understand complex numbers in exponents with his famous Euler’s Formula connecting exponential functions, trigonometric functions, and the field of calculus with complex numbers.
