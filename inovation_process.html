Ferro Fiddles with Cubics


[Mathmatica]

Picture of Scipione del Ferro

Scipione del Ferro, a professor at the University of Bologna was the first to come up with the solution to the cubic equation x^3+ax^2+bx+c=0. However, there were many unsolvable cases where the number of roots was not equal to 3 because he came across the square root of negative numbers.

“If this subject has hitherto been considered from the wrong viewpoint and thus enveloped in mystery and surrounded by darkness, it is largely an unsuitable terminology which should be blamed. Had +1, -1 and √ −1, instead of being called positive, negative and imaginary (or worse still, impossible) unity, been given the names say,of direct, inverse and lateral unity, there would hardly have been any scope for such obscurity.”
~ Carl Friedrich Gauss

Cardano Cracks the Code


Desmos

This is a cubic equation that Cardano would have worked with. Notice how the equation touches the x-axis only once, this means that two out of the three roots are imaginary. Cardano learn how to discard the two imaginary roots and use the real.

Girolamo Cardano, however, found that by excluding the two (what we call imaginary roots) one could find the real root. He provided a method for solving quadratic and cubic equations in his Ars Magna. In the section on postulating a negative, he calls the answer a new number, terming it the “true negative”

"In sixteenth century Italy, there was a major breakthrough in mathematics. Several Italian mathematicians figured out how to solve cubic equations and fourth degree equations as well. But as we see in Girolamo Cardano's Ars Magna (Cardano, 1968), the basic principle here was the same as in the solution of quadratic equations."
~Victor J. Katz

Bombelli Accnoledges Complex Numbers

Rafael Bombelli provided methods to perform operations on complex numbers. In his Algebra, he attempted to prove imaginary numbers using constructions in two and three dimensions, believing that there must be a geometric proof for his newfound class of numbers. Although unsuccessful, he provided many ideas and a basis for other mathematicians to improve upon.

"Plus by plus of minus, makes plus of minus. [1*i=i]
Minus by plus of minus, makes minus of minus. [-1*i=-i]
Plus by minus of minus, makes minus of minus. [1*-i=-i]
Minus by minus of minus, makes plus of minus. [-1*-i=i]
Plus of minus by plus of minus, makes minus. [i*i=-1]
Plus of minus by minus of minus, makes plus. [i*-i=1]
Minus of minus by plus of minus, makes plus. [-i*i=1]
Minus of minus by minus of minus makes minus." [-i*-i=-1]
~Rafael Bombelli [Translations]
